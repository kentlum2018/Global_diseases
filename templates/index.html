"Climate change is the largest and most comprehensive threat to global health security,” says global change biologist Colin J. Carlson, PhD, a postdoctoral fellow in Georgetown University’s biology department. The earth’s climate has constantly been changing over time. The current period of warming is occurring more rapidly than past decades. Scientists are concerned that the natural fluctuation, or variability, is being overtaken by a rapid human-induced warming that has serious implications for the stability of the planet's climate. With this significant shift in global temperature more people than ever before may be newly exposed to disease carrying mosquitoes.

According to the World Health Organization, mosquitoes are one of the deadliest animals in the world, carrying diseases that cause millions of deaths every year. With this continuous change, diseases that we think of as strictly tropical may show up more often in areas with suitable climates. More severe climate change would produce proportionally worse population exposures.

Malaria


Malaria is a mosquito-borne infectious disease that affects humans and other animals. Malaria causes symptoms that typically include fever, tiredness, vomiting, and headaches. In severe cases it can cause yellow skin, seizures, coma, or death.

The WHO estimates that in 2015 there were 214 million new cases of malaria resulting in 438,000 deaths. There are about 10,000 malaria cases per year in Western Europe, and 1300–1500 in the United States. About 900 people died from the disease in Europe between 1993 and 2003. According to the WHO and UNICEF, deaths attributable to malaria in 2015 were reduced by 60% from a 2000 estimate. Malaria is presently endemic in a broad band around the equator, in areas of the Americas, many parts of Asia, and much of Africa; in Sub-Saharan Africa, 85–90% of malaria fatalities occur.


Yellow Fever


Yellow Fever is transmitted by infected mosquitos. Once contracted, the yellow fever virus incubates in the body for 3 to 6 days.

Forty-seven countries in Africa are either endemic for, or have regions that are endemic for, yellow fever. An estimated 84 000–110 000 severe cases of yellow fever were reported in 2013 leading to 29 000–60 000 deaths.


Leprosy


Leprosy is a chronic infectious disease.In 2016, there were 216,108 new leprosy cases registered, corresponding to a global detection rate of 0.29 per 10,000 people.

Although the number of cases worldwide continues to fall, pockets of high prevalence remain in certain areas such as Brazil, South Asia (India, Nepal, Bhutan), some parts of Africa (Tanzania, Madagascar,Mozambique), and the western Pacific. About 150 to 250 cases are diagnosed in the United States each year.


Malaria and Yellow Fever Spread


Malaria and yellow fever are prevalent in tropical and subtropical regions because of rainfall, consistent high temperatures and high humidity, along with stagnant waters where mosquito larvae readily breed. With changing temperature and a global shift in climate, there are more reported cases of Malaria in non tropical regions.
